Fiveleaf is an AI transformation company. We go into a business, listen carefully to what they're trying to do, and deliver AI that helps them do it. That sentence is short for a reason — most of what makes a project succeed or fail sits in the unglamorous bits between those few words.

This is the story of how the company came to be, what we believe about delivering AI well, and what six months of running a live deployment for a UK internet service provider has taught us.

I'll mostly write in *we* — this is a team — but I'm happy to put my name on it. The founder's perspective sits underneath everything below.

— Silviu Major, Founder

## Where Fiveleaf actually came from

Fiveleaf started as a side project. A small web design business, set up a few years ago to make some extra money outside the day job. It wasn't grand and it wasn't strategic. It was the kind of thing a lot of operators have lying around: a domain, a few clients, an evening hobby that paid for itself.

The day job was enterprise software sales. Years of it. Specifically, selling CRM and automation tools to mid-market and enterprise businesses across the UK. The role was account management — the person on the other end of the call after the contract is signed, when the product is in the building and the question becomes whether it'll actually deliver the value the customer was promised.

Account management gets dismissed sometimes as the soft side of software. It isn't. It's where you find out — every day, in detail — what makes technology projects land and what makes them fail. We watched dozens of those projects up close. The ones that worked shared a pattern. The ones that failed shared a different pattern, and the failures were almost never about the technology itself.

In the later years, the work increasingly focused on AI specifically — the early commercial applications, the integrations, the implementations. We were on the inside of that wave for long enough to see it stop being marketing language and start being software that customers actually relied on.

We also got a personal lesson in how serious it was. The founder's role was eventually automated — replaced by AI itself. We don't dwell on that, but it focused the mind. Building AI well, and helping other businesses build it well, looked like a more durable bet than continuing to sell other people's.

That's the origin story. A side business pivoting toward something that combined two things we already knew well: how to deliver software inside a real business, and how AI is actually used at the sharp end of a customer-facing operation.

## Why account management is the foundation of what we do

There's a connection that gets lost when AI projects are scoped: the link between the person who talks to the client every day and the product being delivered. The implementation, the support, the slow week-by-week improvement — none of that lands without that link being strong.

Most AI deployments fail at implementation, not at the technology. The model isn't the problem. The integration isn't really the problem either, even though it's where the symptoms tend to show. The deeper problem is that nobody on the vendor side is close enough to the customer to notice when the deployment has drifted, or when a small change in the customer's operation has made yesterday's tuning wrong, or when the team using the agent has started to lose confidence in it.

We believe in keeping that link short. The founder runs the client relationship. We don't hand customers off to a junior account manager three weeks in. The same person who pitched you the project is the one in your weekly tuning call a year later. It's slower to scale this way; it's also the single biggest reason our clients keep their AI working past the six-month mark when so many others quietly slide into the *we used to have an AI thing* category.

> The relationship is the product. The AI is the part that makes the product work better.

That's an account manager's perspective on a software business. It sounds obvious. It isn't actually how most AI vendors operate.

## What we believe about delivering AI well

A few things we hold to, drawn from what we've seen work.

**Lay strong foundations first.** Before building anything, we map the overall scope of what the business is trying to automate — not just the immediate request. A customer might come in asking for a billing-query agent, but if their data is sitting in three disconnected systems and there's no single customer identifier, the right first move isn't the agent. It's the foundations underneath it. We'd rather spend two weeks getting the basics right than ship something that performs in week one and falls over in week eight.

**Don't try to do too much too fast.** The most common pattern in failed AI projects is over-scoping in the first month. Five channels, ten ticket types, every customer-facing surface — all switched on at once. There's no way to tune that. Testing breaks. Edge cases multiply. The team gets buried in maintenance and the agent never gets good at any one thing. We deliberately start narrow. One channel, two ticket types. Get those excellent. Then add the next two.

**Embed in the business, don't deliver and disappear.** AI deployments need ongoing tuning. The world the agent operates in shifts every month — pricing changes, new products, edge cases the model hadn't seen, tone-of-voice corrections. A vendor that builds and walks away leaves you with an asset that quietly degrades. We stay in the weekly review for as long as the partnership runs, and the deployment gets sharper over time rather than dumber.

**Look beyond the first project.** Once we're in a business, we'll often see other places where AI would help — and we'll say so. Sometimes it's an obvious second workflow, sometimes it's a smaller automation that doesn't even need AI. The point is to be useful to the operation as a whole, not to defend the boundaries of the original statement of work.

These aren't novel ideas. They're the things any good operator already knows about delivering software well. What's surprising is how often AI vendors forget them.

## The proof point: six months in production

We work with a UK internet service provider. They're anonymous in everything we write — that's the partnership. What we *can* share is what the deployment is doing.

The agent has been live in their customer operation for six months. It handles thousands of conversations every month, across customer service and inbound enquiries. It creates product records as part of those conversations — meaning the data captured during a conversation flows into their systems automatically, with no manual rekeying.

The commercial result: in the first six months, the deployment has contributed to over £50,000 of new business closed for the partner. Some of that is captured leads that would otherwise have died unanswered. Some is upsell conversations the agent runs end-to-end and hands off cleanly when they're ready to close. Pound for pound, the engagement is currently returning five times what they're spending with us — a five-to-one monthly return on the partnership.

We're proud of those numbers, but we're more interested in what's underneath them. The reason they exist isn't the model we picked. It's the foundations work in months one and two, the embedded tuning we're still doing every week, and the relationship between the client team and ours.

This is the deployment we know best. We've done our own work, not other people's, and we'd rather speak from what we've actually built than from theory.

## What it costs to work with us

We don't publish a price list. The reason is that pricing depends meaningfully on the shape of the work — how much we're operating, how much we're advising on, how complex the integration is.

The honest benchmark is this: a managed Fiveleaf partnership tends to cost less than hiring one or two in-house AI engineers, and often less than one. A capable in-house AI engineer in the UK runs around £90,000 a year fully loaded — call it £8,000-£10,000 a month. Most businesses that try the in-house route end up needing a second engineer to maintain and improve the agent over time, otherwise it freezes in place and starts under-performing.

For roughly that same money — or less — we'll deliver the build, run it, tune it month-on-month, and keep recommending workflows where AI would help next. No hiring risk, no ramp-up, no week-one panic if the engineer leaves.

If that maths works for your business, the discovery call is the next step. If it doesn't, we'll tell you — there are plenty of businesses for which the right answer is *not yet, get the foundations in place first, then come back*. We don't have the bandwidth to sell projects that won't perform, and selling them wouldn't be good for either of us.

## Where we're going

We're a small team building deliberately. The companies we partner with tend to find us through referral, conversation, and pieces like this one. We're not chasing scale — we're chasing the kind of deployment we can still be in the weekly call for, a year from now, with the same conviction we had on the first call.

If you've read this far and you're considering whether AI could meaningfully change something inside your business, we'd be happy to talk. The first call is thirty minutes, free, and there's no pitch in it — just an honest read on whether it's the right time, the right scope, and whether we'd be the right partner for you.
